TITLE: Gastrointestinal Adverse Events with GLP-1 Receptor Agonists — Clinical Review
DRUG CLASS: GLP-1 receptor agonists

OVERVIEW:
GI adverse events are the most common side effects of GLP-1 RAs, occurring in 30-50% of patients. 
They are the primary reason for treatment discontinuation. Understanding their pattern and 
management is essential for clinical use.

INCIDENCE BY EVENT TYPE (pooled across major trials):
- Nausea: 30-44% with GLP-1 RA vs 8-16% placebo
- Vomiting: 15-25% vs 3-7% placebo
- Diarrhoea: 20-30% vs 10-16% placebo
- Constipation: 10-24% vs 5-11% placebo
- Abdominal pain: 10-15% vs 5-8% placebo
- Dyspepsia: 5-10% vs 2-5% placebo
- GI events leading to discontinuation: 4-7% vs 0.5-1.5% placebo

PATTERN AND TIMING:
- Most GI events occur during dose escalation phase (first 4-16 weeks)
- Severity typically mild to moderate
- Events are transient — resolve with continued treatment in most patients
- Plateau effect — GI events decrease after reaching maintenance dose
- Higher doses associated with higher rates (semaglutide 2.4mg > 1.0mg)

MANAGEMENT STRATEGIES:
- Slow dose escalation — standard 4-week intervals reduce GI burden
- Take injection on same day each week — consistent exposure
- Eat smaller, lower-fat meals during initiation
- Avoid lying down immediately after eating
- Temporary dose reduction if intolerable — return to escalation when tolerated
- Anti-emetics rarely needed but can be used short-term

RISK FACTORS FOR SEVERE GI EVENTS:
- Prior history of gastroparesis — relative contraindication
- Rapid dose escalation
- High-fat diet
- Concurrent use of other agents slowing gastric motility

GASTROPARESIS CONCERN:
- GLP-1 RAs slow gastric emptying — can worsen pre-existing gastroparesis
- Case reports of new-onset gastroparesis — causal relationship not established
- FDA added warning in 2023 regarding gastroparesis risk
- Patients with symptoms of gastroparesis should be evaluated before initiating

CLINICAL IMPLICATIONS:
GI adverse events are expected, manageable, and usually transient. Patient counseling before 
initiation significantly improves adherence. The benefit-risk profile remains favorable for 
most patients. Pre-existing gastroparesis is a relative contraindication requiring careful 
clinical assessment.

SOURCE: Davies M, et al. Diabetes Obes Metab. 2021;23(4):938-946. Systematic review.
