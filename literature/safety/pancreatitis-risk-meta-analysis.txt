TITLE: Pancreatitis Risk with GLP-1 Receptor Agonists — Meta-Analysis Review
DRUG CLASS: GLP-1 receptor agonists

BACKGROUND:
Pancreatitis was identified as a potential concern with GLP-1 RAs based on preclinical data 
showing pancreatic ductal hyperplasia in rodent models. This prompted regulatory scrutiny 
and dedicated safety analyses across all major CVOTs.

EVIDENCE FROM MAJOR TRIALS:
- LEADER (liraglutide): 18 vs 23 pancreatitis events — not increased with liraglutide
- SUSTAIN-6 (semaglutide): 5 vs 2 events — numerically higher but not significant (small numbers)
- SELECT (semaglutide 2.4mg): no increased pancreatitis signal reported
- EXSCEL (exenatide): similar rates between arms
- ELIXA (lixisenatide): similar rates between arms

META-ANALYSIS FINDINGS:
- Pooled analysis of all GLP-1 RA CVOTs: no statistically significant increase in pancreatitis
- Relative risk approximately 1.0-1.2 — not significant
- Absolute event rates low in both arms across all trials
- No dose-response relationship demonstrated

REGULATORY STATUS:
- FDA label includes pancreatitis as a warning for all GLP-1 RAs
- Warning based on preclinical data and case reports, not CVOT evidence
- EMA similarly maintains pancreatitis warning
- Labeled as: discontinue if pancreatitis suspected, do not restart if confirmed

CLINICAL GUIDANCE:
- Prior history of pancreatitis: relative contraindication — exercise caution
- Active pancreatitis: contraindicated
- Patients with risk factors (gallstones, heavy alcohol use, hypertriglyceridaemia): 
  assess benefit-risk carefully
- Routine pancreatic enzyme monitoring not recommended in asymptomatic patients

CONTRAST WITH PRECLINICAL DATA:
- Rodent studies showed pancreatic ductal changes not replicated in humans
- Human pancreatic pathology studies (post-mortem) did not confirm ductal hyperplasia
- FDA and EMA reviewed raw pancreatic pathology data — no human safety signal confirmed

CLINICAL IMPLICATIONS:
The pancreatitis risk from GLP-1 RAs appears to be a class label warning not supported by 
robust clinical trial evidence. However, the labeled contraindication for active or recent 
pancreatitis should be respected. The benefit-risk ratio for most patients remains strongly 
favorable given the CV and metabolic benefits demonstrated in large RCTs.

SOURCE: Monami M, et al. Diabetes Obes Metab. 2014;16(10):938-943. Meta-analysis.
